Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

Simply understanding that your brain is malleable is the first step to increasing brain function. This is because it immediately changes the way you think and approach learning.

Many online and scholarly sources state that there are ten principles to neuroplasticity. These are:

1. Use it or lose it
Lack of practice over an extended period of time weakens neural pathways.

2. Use it and improve it
WIth a lot of practice over time, neural pathways strengthen.

3. Salience
If the practice you are doing is made meaningful/interesting/important to you, new skills and information are easier to hold on to.

4. Repetition
This is simple. The more a thought or action is repeated, the more it sticks.

5. Intensity
The effort put into learning has a great impact on the quality of the learning. This includes increasing intensity over time.

6. Specificity
Training or learning should be specific to what your intended outcome is.

7. Age
The younger a person is, the more malleable their brain is. In other words, it is easier to learn when you are younger.

8. Time
There are ‘windows of opportunity’ when your brain is more receptive to learning.

9. Transference
Learning in one area that strengthens certain neural pathways can activate nearby neural pathways. This means the training of one skill can transfer to another skill.

10. Interference
Your brain can learn bad habits as easily as it can learn good habits.

This shows that there are many factors that cause changes in your brain. Being aware of what they are and how they might interact with each other is important when wanting to increase brain function.

By grouping some of these principles together, you can benefit your learning greatly. Use it or lose it, use it and improve it, repetition and time can be grouped together to create a learning plan. Use it or lose it and use it and improve it are two sides of the same coin, and fit in nicely with repetition. Practice, practice, practice and pick a specific time when your brain is open to learning. Then you can look at some other principles individually. Make sure there is intensity and specificity to your learning to engage the neural pathways in your brain and make sure it is meaningful to you for salience. While you are learning, stay on task to avoid interference. The transference aspect of neuroplasticity comes naturally, as you will find similar tasks will become easier. The final principle is age. Although it may seem like this principle suggests you may be too old to learn something new, the takeaway from it should be that the earlier you learn something the better. Start today and not tomorrow. Following a plan like this and putting in some work will help greatly in increasing neuroplasticity.

Growth Mindset

The growth mindset can best be described with one word, ‘yet’. A major thing holding a lot of people back from long-term learning goals is a negative view towards not knowing something. The growth mindset is the idea that you shouldn’t say to yourself or others that you don’t know something or that you’ve failed something. You should instead say that you haven’t learnt it yet or you haven’t passed it yet. To elaborate further, people with a growth mindset believe that just because you don’t have a certain skill, it doesn’t mean that you can’t learn it. A growth mindset isn’t something that you are born with. It can be learnt and utilised by anyone. The opposite of the growth mindset is the fixed mindset. As the name suggests, people with a fixed mindset believe that skills, intelligence and the ability to learn are fixed in a person.

Some key differences between the two are the way they deal with facing challenges, putting in effort and receiving criticism. People with a growth mindset face challenges head on and see the positives in them. They see the value in putting in effort and often learn to enjoy it even if it is something they usually wouldn’t choose to do. Finally, they see criticism as something to learn from. On the other hand, people with a fixed mindset try to avoid challenges whenever possible. They may not see the value in putting in effort, as they believe it won’t change the way things are. Finally, they ignore criticism or try to avoid it entirely and don’t see the positives they could take from it.

Through exploring the two opposing mindsets, I realised that at times I have displayed a fixed mindset towards certain things and it has probably held me back. I can think of a number of times at school and university that I didn’t try my hardest when working on a project because I didn’t think I would be capable of learning everything I had to or producing the perfect end product. On the other hand, I can also think of times when I showed a growth mindset, put my mind to learning something and I succeeded in my goals. This shows that while I am capable of utilising a growth mindset, I can just as easily fall into the trap of being stuck in a fixed mindset.

I think I will find it fairly easy to incorporate a growth mindset into my learning journey. Firstly, signing up for Dev Academy and showing the desire to learn is already an example of the mindset. Moving forward I am going to keep myself from being too worried about not understanding concepts, remembering details or getting things correct immediately. Not being able to understand a concept in web development is extremely common for beginners and as I move forward I know that I will find these concepts more and more manageable.

My Learning Plan and Strategies

The concepts of neuroplasticity and the growth mindset will blend well into my learning plan and how I strategise in the future. In my learning plan I wrote that I want to get better at time management. This is something that I have struggled with for a long time and even in the last few weeks, I haven’t completely gotten on top of it. Even though I haven’t learnt to manage my time as well as I would like to, using the growth mindset I know that in the future I am more than capable of doing so. A specific principle that I am going to incorporate into my strategies is repetition. Repeating some of the concepts, ideas and skills that I find difficult now will help them come a lot easier as time goes on.
